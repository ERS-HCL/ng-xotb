Tone of Voice
Voice and tone refers to how we sound when we speak or write.

Voice and tone are generally confused and used interchangeably. However they are very different.

  • Voice refers to our personality that does not change with context or situation.
  • Tone refers to author’s mood. In speech, voice is reflected through pitch, volume, body language, etc.

When we write, it comes across through usage of italics, bold ,capitals, punctuation, grammar, word choice, and just about every other ingredient involved in writing.

Our voice is an expression of our brand’s personality. It represents core principles that guide our communications – the way our messages ‘sounds and feels’ distinctly as HCL Technologies. A congruent, recognizable voice supports and complements our uniform and consistent visual identity.

While the exact tone to be used would depend on the circumstances in consideration, our voice must always be confident, authentic, and simple to understand.

Confident But Not Boasful
  • Write with clarity as it expresses your knowledge.
  • Know what you want to say. Always write down the objective/ purpose of writing in your draft copy before you begin.
  • Sometimes the intent could be to guide the user to take appropriate action.
  • At other times, it could be to merely share the knowledge.
  • In both the cases the tone, way of presenting the content will differ. In the first, it would be more persuasive, with related key information, facts that support the action to be taken. While it the later it would be mere presentation of data.
  • Avoid using jargon and unfamiliar words. IF used, define unfamiliar words to provide clarity.
  • Write one-sentence paragraphs. This will make the content scannable. Single sentence paragraph makes user to stop, read and understand.
  • Keep your sentences short. This method will force you to remove unnecessary information and make your content more crisp and clear. To achieve this, watch out for these words:
    • Adverbs: For example, actually, currently, really, literally etc.
    • Adjectives: very, real, simple etc.
    • Unnecessary words or filler words: perhaps, pretty, now, that, in order, just and maybe
  • Be consistent.
  • Write with Optimism. Write in a hopeful; cheerful. For example, We navigate organizations from where they are to where they aspire to be.
Respectful But Not Condescending
  • Be polite, kind and courteous.
  • Say ‘Thank you’ or ‘Please’.
  • Choose a positive tone of voice over a negative or authoritative tone. For example, instead of using ‘Don’t forget’ use ‘Remember’.
  • Rather than focusing on the problem, focus on the solution or action. For example, instead of ‘Since you didn’t submit your receipts, you cannot claim the reimbursement’ use ‘As soon as you submit your receipts, we will be happy to reimburse the amount’.
  • Avoid long explanations, and focus on the solution
  • Use antonyms to avoid the word “not.” For example, Instead of ‘To avoid taking more time to access key information, use quick links’ use ‘Quick links help you access key
    • information faster’.
    • Instead of saying ‘You are not allowed to swipe in after 10 am’ use ‘Swipe in before 10 am’.
    • Instead of ‘not able’ use ‘unable’
  • Try not to be condescending. Avoid over explaining a concept that people clearly already understand.
  • Avoid patronizing. Talking down makes people feel disrespected.
  • Avoid defensive tone. Be watchful of usage of the word “but”. Agree to disagree..
  • Be authentic
    1. Do thorough research and back your insights with data and facts
    2. Provide social proofs, references wherever possible
  • Create original content, do not indulge in plagiarism
  • Be genuine
    1. Apologize if an error occur.
    2. Appreciate when something is completed or done well by your users. This encourages the users. However don’t overpraise at it might sound unreal.
    3. Use simple, clear sentences instead of using jargon-heavy language to create a sense of superiority.
Friendly But Not Casual
  • Write in a conversational tone
    1. Use active voice over a passive one.
    2. Instead of ‘employees’ or ‘users’, use ‘You’ and ‘we’
    3. Keep sentences short, clear and jargon light.
    4. Use contractions. For example, instead of ‘they will’ and ‘we are’ use they’ll and we’re over to make it sound less formal.
    5. Use interjections such as Yeh! Oh-Oh! Sorry! Thanks! However, avoid using colloquial slangs that makes it sound too casual.
    6. Avoid using unnecessary heavy jargons. Use simple words that are easy to consume.
    7. Use more emotional or sensory words to add power to your message. However, avoid being too dramatic.

When crafting communications, it would be likely to have the same voice all the time, but our tone would differ depending on where we are, who we are with and the emotions and expectations of our audiences.

Such slight variations in tone of our communications can make it easier for us to connect with our audiences.

The factors which may affect the tone of our communications include, but would not be limited to:

  • The intended audience
  • Medium of communication
  • And context
